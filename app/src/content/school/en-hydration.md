Hydration is the most quoted number in modern baking: water as a percentage of flour weight. A bagel sits near 55%, a sandwich loaf near 65%, a country sourdough near 75%, a ciabatta at 80-plus. The number is not a skill rating. It is a **style choice** with physical consequences.

At low hydration (55–65%), dough is firm and obedient. It holds sharp shapes, takes fine even crumb, and browns to a thicker, sturdier crust. This is bagel, pretzel and pan-loaf territory, and the pizza napoletana canon lives here too, drier than most home bakers expect.

Push toward 70–80% and everything loosens. Gluten organizes into a more open web; the crumb goes irregular and glossy; the crust thins and crackles. The dough demands better technique, folds instead of kneading, tension built gently, flour used sparingly on the bench. This is the artisan hearth-loaf zone.

Above 85% you are in ciabatta and pan de cristal country: batter-adjacent doughs, coil folds, wet hands, cold retards for handling, and a crumb like stained glass when it works. Wetter is not better, it is simply *another bread*, harder to shape and quicker to collapse when underdeveloped.

Two footnotes keep the number honest. **Wholegrain drinks**: bran and germ absorb water that never reaches the gluten, so a 75% wholegrain dough handles like a 68% white one, raise hydration when you raise extraction. And **rye rewrites the rules entirely**: its pentosans bind water into a paste, so a 100% rye at 90% hydration is normal and firm-ish, while wheat at 90% is an expert's slack dough. Compare hydration only within a family, never across it.

When designing a dough in the Lab, start from the style's range, not from ambition. The dough does not know what number you were aiming for, only how it feels.
