Everything alive in your dough obeys the thermometer. Commercial yeast is a single domesticated organism selected for speed and CO₂; a sourdough culture is a working village, wild yeasts for lift, **lactic acid bacteria** for depth, and both communities take their orders from temperature.

The rule of thumb worth memorizing: fermentation speed roughly **doubles or halves with every 8 °C** of dough temperature. A bulk that takes 4 hours at 25 °C wants closer to 8 at 17 °C. This is why serious recipes state a dough temperature rather than a kitchen-clock time, and why the Lab computes your water temperature from it.

For sourdough there is a second, finer dial: **which acid you brew**. Warm doughs (28–35 °C) favor homofermentative bacteria producing lactic acid, yogurt-mild, round, creamy. Cooler, stiffer, longer ferments (18–26 °C) shift toward acetic acid, the vinegar-bright tang of a real San Francisco loaf. "More sour" is not a matter of more time alone; it is a matter of *which* time at *which* temperature. Too mild? Retard colder and longer, keep the starter stiffer. Too sharp? Ferment warmer, wetter, younger.

Judging doneness is the craft's hardest skill because the clock lies. An underproofed dough is dense, tears when stretched and bursts wildly in the oven; an overproofed one is fragile, slack, full of large sighing bubbles, and bakes flat with a pale, sad crust. The honest tests are physical: volume gain (roughly half again for most wheat doughs), a domed jiggle, bubbles at the edges, and the poke test, a gentle dimple that springs back slowly, but not instantly.

Preferments are fermentation borrowed in advance. A **poolish** (100% hydration, a pinch of yeast, overnight) brings extensibility and hazelnut sweetness; a **biga** (stiff, Italian) brings strength and aroma; a **levain** brings the whole village. Ten to thirty percent of prefermented flour is bread's cheapest flavor upgrade.

The dough decides, not the clock, the clock is just a forecast.
