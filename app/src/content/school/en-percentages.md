Recipes tell you *amounts*; formulas tell you *relationships*. Baker's math is the convention that makes the relationship visible: **total flour is always 100%**, and every other ingredient is expressed against it. Water 75%, salt 2%, levain 20%. Written this way, a recipe scales to any size in one multiplication, and two breads from different books become instantly comparable.

A worked example. You want two 900 g loaves: 1800 g of dough. Your formula is 75% water, 2% salt, 20% levain (at 100% hydration). The percentage sum is 100 + 75 + 2 = 177, the levain is *not* added to the sum, because its flour and water are already part of the totals. So total flour = 1800 ÷ 1.77 ≈ **1017 g**, water = **763 g**, salt = **20 g**. The levain weighs 20% of flour ≈ 203 g, and since it is half flour, half water, you subtract ~102 g from each of the final flour and water additions. This bookkeeping, counting the flour *inside* the preferment, is what professionals mean by **prefermented flour percentage**, and it is the convention this entire atlas uses.

One more number turns the formula into a schedule: **desired dough temperature**. Flour, room and mixing friction all deposit heat into the dough, but only the water is under your control on bake day. The classic accounting: water temp = (DDT × 3) − room − flour − friction, with a ×4 multiplier when a preferment joins the equation. Friction is real, a few degrees by hand, twelve or more in a stand mixer.

Why bother? Because percentages are how you *think* in bread rather than merely follow it. Want the same loaf wetter? Move one number. Swapping 20% of the flour to rye? The formula shows exactly what else must move. The Dough Lab does this arithmetic live, but the point is that after a while, you will do it in your head, and every bread ever written becomes a variation you can read at a glance.
