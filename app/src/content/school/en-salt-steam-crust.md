**Salt** does three unrelated jobs at once, which is why nearly every bread on earth lands between 1.8 and 2.2% of flour weight. It brakes fermentation by osmotic pressure, giving flavor time to develop. It tightens gluten, turning a slack dough noticeably stronger within minutes. And it seasons, not just savoriness, but the difference between bread that tastes like wheat and bread that tastes like nothing. Below ~1.5% doughs race ahead and taste flat; above ~2.5% fermentation drags visibly. The famous exception, Tuscany's pane sciocco, proves the rule: saltless bread was built for salty food.

**Steam** is the crust's negotiator. For the first phase of the bake, the loaf wants to expand violently, *oven spring*, as trapped gas heats and water turns to vapor. A dry oven sets the crust in the first minutes and caps that expansion; scores stay shut, the loaf shrugs. Steam keeps the surface below ~100 °C, supple and gelatinized: the loaf opens its scores, an ear lifts, and the surface later dries into gloss and crackle. This is the entire logic of the dutch oven, a pot is simply a steam room the loaf carries with it.

Then the negotiation must end. Around the fifteen-minute mark you vent the steam (or lift the lid), because browning, the **Maillard reaction**, only begins in earnest above ~120 °C on a *dry* surface. Steam too long and the crust stays pale and leathery; too short and it never opened. Fifteen minutes wet, the rest dry, is the spine of most hearth-bread schedules.

Finally: bake bolder than your nerves suggest. The deep-brown crust registers as flavor, caramel, malt, roast, and an underbaked "golden" loaf is mostly a missed opportunity. Lean breads are done at 96–99 °C inside; enriched doughs, protected by sugar and fat, finish lower, around 82–88 °C.
