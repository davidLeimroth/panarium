Flour looks inert. It is not. Every bag carries two numbers that quietly decide how your bake will go: **protein** and **extraction**.

Protein (roughly 9–11% in all-purpose, 12–14% in bread flour) becomes **gluten** when wet: glutenin gives elasticity, gliadin gives stretch. Together they build the mesh that traps fermentation gas. More protein means more water absorbed, more strength, more chew; less protein means tenderness. Neither is "better", a baguette wants moderate strength, a panettone demands a flour so strong it can carry half its weight in butter.

**Extraction** is how much of the grain made it into the bag. White flour is the starchy heart of the kernel; high-extraction and wholegrain flours keep the outer layers, more flavor, more minerals, and sharp bran flakes that both drink water and slice through gluten strands. That is why wholegrain doughs want extra hydration, gentler handling and often a touch more fermentation time, and why they repay you with flavor white flour cannot reach.

Then there is **rye**, which plays a different sport entirely. Rye has little gluten to speak of; its structure comes from **pentosans**, gums that bind many times their weight in water and produce a sticky paste rather than an elastic dough. Above roughly 40% rye you stop kneading and start *mixing*, you lean on sourdough acidity to keep the starches stable, and you accept a denser, moister, longer-keeping crumb as the entire point.

Spelt and the ancient grains, einkorn, emmer, khorasan, sit in between: real gluten, but fragile. They hydrate fast, ferment enthusiastically and overproof without warning, which is why recipes treat them gently.

Read a recipe's flour list first. It tells you what the dough will want from your hands before you have touched it.
